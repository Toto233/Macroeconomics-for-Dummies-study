# Chapter 2 Overview

## Theme

Chapter 2 introduces the main questions macroeconomists care about and explains the modelling mindset used to study them.

## Main Thread

The chapter first frames the big macro questions: why countries grow, why prices rise, and why unemployment exists. It then explains why economists use models, why intuition can mislead, why macro should rest on microfoundations, and ends by clarifying core language such as real versus nominal, stock versus flow, levels versus growth rates, and interest rates.

## Core Concepts

1. Long-run growth matters because small growth-rate differences compound over time.
2. Inflation is the growth rate of the price level.
3. Deflation can weaken demand by encouraging delayed spending.
4. Economists use simplified models to isolate the mechanism that matters.
5. Good macro models should have microfoundations.
6. Real variables differ from nominal variables.
7. Income is a flow, while wealth is a stock.
8. A variable's level is different from its growth rate.
9. The interest rate is the price of money and affects both consumption and investment.

## Key Causal Chains

1. Higher growth rate -> compounded over many years -> much higher long-run living standards.
2. Higher expected future price declines -> delayed spending -> weaker demand -> more downward pressure on prices.
3. Higher interest rate -> saving becomes more attractive and borrowing more expensive -> lower consumption and investment.

## Common Confusions

- High growth does not mean high level.
- Inflation is not the same thing as the price level.
- Real is not the same thing as nominal.
- Income is not the same thing as wealth.
- Stock variables are not the same as flow variables.
- GDP, output, production, income, and expenditure are different labels for the same economy-wide total when measured correctly.

## Connection Forward

This chapter gives the language and logic needed for later chapters on GDP, inflation, unemployment, interest rates, and macro policy.
