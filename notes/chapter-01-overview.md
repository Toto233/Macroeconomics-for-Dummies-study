# Chapter 1 Overview

## Theme

Chapter 1 explains why macroeconomics matters, what macroeconomists look at, and why they rely on simplified models to understand a complex economy.

## Main Thread

The chapter starts from the big picture: macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole rather than individual markets. It then introduces the main variables macroeconomists watch, explains why economists build models, and connects diagnosis to policy and crises.

## Core Concepts

1. Macroeconomics focuses on economy-wide outcomes.
2. GDP tracks total output and overall economic activity.
3. Economic growth is different from the level of GDP.
4. Nominal GDP and real GDP are different tools.
5. Inflation tracks how fast prices are rising.
6. Unemployment tracks how hard it is for people to find work.
7. Models are simplified versions of reality used to isolate the key mechanism in a problem.

## Common Confusions

- GDP is not the same thing as GDP growth.
- Real GDP is not a perfectly "true" number; it is an adjusted measure for comparison over time.
- Inflation is the rate of price increase, not simply "prices are high."
- A good model does not include everything; it includes what matters for the question being asked.

## Modelling the Economy

- The book presents models as simplified versions of reality.
- Their purpose is not to copy the whole world but to help economists think through complicated problems.
- A model is useful when it keeps the factors that matter for the question and ignores the ones that do not.
- The book's example is cross-country average wages: if the question is why average wages differ across countries, it can be useful to ignore wage differences across individuals within each country.
